# Tokyo Travel Guide — Contoso Travel

## Overview
Tokyo is a city where ancient temples sit beside neon-lit skyscrapers, where you can eat the world's best sushi for $10 and shop in stores that feel like they're from the future. It's organized chaos at its finest.

## Neighborhoods

### Shibuya
The iconic scramble crossing is just the beginning. Shibuya is Tokyo's youth culture hub with incredible shopping, nightlife, and street fashion. Don't miss Center-gai for people-watching and Nonbei Yokocho ("Drunkard's Alley") for tiny atmospheric bars.

**Best for:** Nightlife, shopping, first-time visitors
**Getting there:** JR Yamanote Line to Shibuya Station

### Shinjuku
Home to the busiest train station in the world. East Shinjuku is neon lights and entertainment (including the famous Golden Gai bar district with 200+ tiny bars). West Shinjuku is corporate skyscrapers with free observation decks.

**Best for:** Entertainment, photography, budget nightlife
**Getting there:** Any major JR line to Shinjuku Station

### Asakusa
Tokyo's most traditional neighborhood. Senso-ji temple (Tokyo's oldest) is the centerpiece, surrounded by Nakamise-dori shopping street selling traditional crafts and snacks. The area along the Sumida River is perfect for evening walks.

**Best for:** Culture, temples, traditional shopping
**Getting there:** Ginza Line or Asakusa Line to Asakusa Station

### Akihabara
Electric Town — the epicenter of anime, manga, and gaming culture. Multi-story arcades, maid cafés, and electronics stores as far as the eye can see. Even if you're not into anime, the energy is infectious.

**Best for:** Anime fans, electronics, unique experiences
**Getting there:** JR Yamanote Line to Akihabara Station

### Harajuku & Omotesando
Harajuku's Takeshita Street is teen fashion central (crepes, costumes, crowds). Walk 5 minutes to Omotesando for the complete opposite — Tokyo's Champs-Élysées with luxury brands and architectural masterpieces.

**Best for:** Fashion, photography, contrast between pop and luxury
**Getting there:** JR Yamanote Line to Harajuku Station

## Food Guide

### Ramen
- **Fuunji (Shinjuku):** Famous tsukemen (dipping ramen). Line starts at 11am. Worth it. ~¥1,000
- **Ichiran (multiple locations):** Solo-booth ramen — customize everything on a paper form. Tourist-friendly, consistently excellent. ~¥1,000
- **Rokurinsha (Tokyo Station):** Inside Ramen Street in the basement. Rich, thick seafood broth tsukemen. Long lines but they move fast. ~¥1,100

### Sushi
- **Sushi Dai (Toyosu Market):** Worth the early morning trek. Omakase set for ~¥4,000 is extraordinary value. Arrive by 5am.
- **Standing sushi bars in Shibuya:** Quick, fresh, affordable. ~¥1,500–2,500 for a filling meal.
- **Conveyor belt sushi (Genki Sushi):** Fun, affordable, and better quality than you'd expect. ~¥1,000–2,000

### Street Food
- **Takoyaki (octopus balls):** Best at festivals and street carts in Asakusa. ~¥500
- **Melon pan:** Sweet bread with cookie crust. Get it fresh from bakeries in Harajuku. ~¥200
- **Yakitori under the tracks:** Yurakucho's yakitori alley under the JR tracks — smoky, atmospheric, incredible grilled chicken. ~¥1,500 with drinks

## Practical Tips

### Transport
- **Get a Suica/Pasmo IC card** at any station machine. Works on all trains, buses, and many convenience stores. Much easier than buying individual tickets.
- **JR Yamanote Line** is the loop line connecting all major areas. Free with Japan Rail Pass.
- **Avoid rush hour** (7:30–9:30am) on trains — it's as packed as the videos show.
- **Last train** is around midnight. After that: night buses, taxis (~¥3,000–5,000 for typical rides), or karaoke until 5am when trains restart.

### Money
- **Japan is still cash-heavy.** Many small restaurants and shops don't accept cards. Carry at least ¥10,000 in cash.
- **7-Eleven ATMs** accept international cards reliably. Other ATMs may not.
- **No tipping** — it's considered rude. Service charges are included.

### Etiquette
- **Quiet on trains** — no phone calls, keep conversations low
- **Remove shoes** when entering homes, some restaurants, and temples
- **Don't eat while walking** — find a bench or eat at the shop
- **Bow slightly** when greeting people — it goes a long way
